To calculate the electric potential at the center of the square, follow these steps:

### Step 1: Identify the Position of the Charges
The charges are placed at the corners of a square with side length $s = 1.0 \text{ m}$. In a coordinate system where the center is $(0,0)$, the corners are located at $(\pm 0.5, \pm 0.5)$.

### Step 2: Calculate the Distance to the Center
Electric potential depends on the distance $r$ from the source charge to the point of interest. For the center of a square, the distance to any corner is half the length of the diagonal.
Using the Pythagorean theorem for a side $s$:
$$\text{Diagonal} = s\sqrt{2} = 1.0\sqrt{2} \approx 1.414 \text{ m}$$
$$r = \frac{s\sqrt{2}}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \approx 0.707 \text{ m}$$



### Step 3: Recall the Formula for Electric Potential
The potential $V$ created by a single point charge $q$ is:
$$V = \frac{kq}{r}$$
Where $k$ (Coulomb's constant) is approximately $8.99 \times 10^9 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}^2$.

### Step 4: Sum the Potentials (Superposition Principle)
Unlike electric force (which is a vector), electric potential is a **scalar**. This means you simply add the values together algebraically without worrying about angles or directions.
Since $r$ is the same for all four charges, we can factor out $\frac{k}{r}$:
$$V_{total} = \frac{k}{r}(q_1 + q_2 + q_3 + q_4)$$

### Step 5: Plug in the Values
The charges are $+1\text{C}, -2\text{C}, +3\text{C},$ and $-4\text{C}$.
1.  **Sum of charges:** $1 - 2 + 3 - 4 = -2 \text{ C}$
2.  **Calculation:**
$$V_{total} = \frac{8.99 \times 10^9}{0.707} \times (-2)$$
$$V_{total} \approx (1.27 \times 10^{10}) \times (-2)$$
$$V_{total} \approx -2.54 \times 10^{10} \text{ V}$$

The negative result indicates that the net work required to bring a positive test charge from infinity to the center of this specific configuration would be negative (the field does the work).
